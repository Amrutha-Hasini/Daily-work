Priya and the Empty Purse Trick

Priya was a young working woman in a bustling city. Every evening, she boarded the crowded local bus after office. She often carried her laptop and some cash — just enough to get by. One late night, the bus was nearly empty. Priya sat near the window, scrolling through her phone. At the next stop, two men boarded and sat near her. Something felt wrong. Their eyes kept darting toward her bag. Her instincts whispered: Be careful. As the bus grew quieter, one of them leaned closer. “Hand over your wallet and phone,” he hissed, his tone threatening. Priya’s heart thumped loudly in her chest. She looked around. The driver was far ahead, unaware. No one else was in sight. Running wasn’t an option. Crying for help would only agitate them. Her mind raced. Think, Priya. Use your brain. Slowly, she reached into her handbag and pulled out a small purse. But it wasn’t her real one — just an old, nearly empty pouch. Calmly, she held it out and said, “This is all I have. Please take it, just don’t hurt me.” The men snatched the purse and opened it. Inside were only a few coins and a faded ID card. They muttered in frustration, glaring at her. Priya didn’t flinch. She forced her face into helplessness, while secretly sliding her real wallet and phone deeper into a hidden zipper. As the bus slowed at the next stop, she took a deep breath. She pointed out the window and shouted suddenly, “Look! Police outside!” Startled, the men glanced out instinctively. In that split second, Priya darted toward the front of the bus, calling loudly to the driver, “Stop! Help!” The driver immediately pulled over. The men, realizing attention was now on them, jumped off the bus and ran into the darkness. Priya trembled, clutching her bag tightly. Her real belongings were safe. Her mind, not her strength, had saved her. Later, when she narrated the incident at home, her mother hugged her and said: “You proved that courage is not always about fighting. Sometimes, it’s about staying calm and outsmarting danger.” From that day, Priya carried two purses whenever she traveled. One real, one fake — her little shield against the unknown. Her story became popular in her office. Colleagues said, “You’ve taught us how to be street-smart.” And Priya herself often reminds others: “In a crisis, fear is natural. But if you let your mind lead, you can turn even fear into a clever weapon.”
